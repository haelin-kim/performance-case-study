Case Study · People Operations

Building an AI-first performance system, as a non-engineer

I designed and shipped a performance review and management program for a 30-person tech company. The goal was to automate the administrative load so managers could spend their time on judgment instead of paperwork.

Role: Sole designer and builder Team: 2-person People Ops function Stack: Slack, Google Apps Script, AI pair programmer Timeline: Shipped in under 3 months

01 · Context

A growing company that had outgrown informal reviews

The company had roughly 30 employees and was growing deliberately, with no plan to add headcount to the People function. Reviews happened, but inconsistently: some managers ran thoughtful check-ins, others did little, and there was no shared record leadership could look at to compare across teams.

Two forces raised the stakes. First, the company was adopting AI agents into daily work, which kept shifting the definition of a "high performing" employee. Second, the entire program had to be designed and run by a two-person People Ops team, neither of whom was an engineer. Whatever I built had to be maintainable by people who write no code for a living.

02 · The Problem

Rigorous enough to defend, light enough to actually use

Performance programs at this size tend to fail in one of two directions. Too heavy, and they become a bureaucracy managers quietly ignore. Too light, and they turn into a vibes-based process that cannot defend a promotion or support a difficult performance conversation.

I needed a program that sat between those failure modes: structured enough to stand behind, and light enough that busy managers would genuinely use it. It also had to identify high performers early, surface performance issues before they hardened, and do all of this without a dedicated operator babysitting the process each cycle.

03 · Approach

Agent-First, Human-Second

Let automation carry the process. Reserve people for judgment.

My core design principle was that the system should run the cadence on its own, and people should only touch the parts that actually require a person. Software watches the clock, drafts the scaffolding, nudges the stragglers, and logs what is done. A human reviews a digest and handles the exceptions that get flagged. I pressure-tested every step of the workflow against one question: does this really need a person, or does it just feel like it does?

1
Open the cycleAutomated
A single launch action opens the monthly or quarterly cycle, pre-creates records for everyone in scope, and posts prompts directly in Slack where people already work.
2
Collect & draftAutomated
Self-reviews, manager reviews, and peer feedback flow in through forms. The system parses, structures, and files each response, and generates draft scaffolding to lower the blank-page cost.
3
Monitor & nudgeAutomated
The system tracks completion in real time, nudges managers who are behind, and escalates stalled cases instead of letting them silently rot.
4
Review the digestHuman
People Ops reviews a live dashboard: who's done, how ratings are distributed, which cases need attention. No chasing spreadsheets.
5
Handle the exceptionsHuman
Humans spend their time only where judgment matters: flagged reviews, calibration debates, promotion and comp decisions.

04 · The Solution

One connected system, not a pile of forms

The program spans the full performance lifecycle. I built each piece to hand off cleanly to the next, so a review that comes in feeds calibration, which feeds promotion and compensation decisions, with no re-keying of data along the way.

🔁

Review cycles in Slack

Monthly check-ins and quarterly reviews launch and collect where the team already lives. Lighter quarters are notes-only; heavier quarters add structured ratings, so the process flexes to the moment.

📊

Live performance dashboard

Completion tracking, rating distributions, supplementary stats, and a per-person roster with drill-in detail and prior-quarter context, all in one browser-openable view.

🔒

Role-based access control

Admins see the whole org, managers see only their reporting tree, and individual contributors don't see others' data. Privacy enforced by design, not by trust.

🎯

Calibration workspace

A drag-and-drop workspace for promotion decisions with per-person notes, tenure and time-in-role context, and multi-select filters, plus a one-click snapshot export for the meeting record.

💬

Evergreen peer feedback

Anyone can give a colleague feedback anytime, with a public/private choice, delivered straight to the recipient. Feedback stops being a once-a-year event.

💰

Comp adjustment flow

A manager-facing intake plus a writable dashboard, indexed by cycle, that turns raise and adjustment requests into a tracked, auditable process instead of scattered DMs.

06 · How I Built It

No-code instincts, low-code tools, AI as the pair programmer

I am a People Ops professional, not an engineer. I built this by pairing with an AI coding assistant, learning enough of the stack to make sound architectural calls and hand off the implementation. This is increasingly the shape of modern operational work: you do not need to write every line to ship real software.

SlackWhere cycles launch, prompts land, and nudges go, meeting people in their existing workflow.
Google Apps ScriptThe automation backbone: parsing responses, scheduling, access logic, and dashboard data.
Forms & SheetsFamiliar, zero-training data capture and a single source of truth per cycle.
AI coding assistantMy pair programmer for turning design decisions into working, maintainable code.

What I brought was the systems thinking: the workflow design, the access model, the calibration logic, and the judgment about what to automate versus keep human. The AI accelerated the build. The design decisions were mine.

07 · Problems We Targeted

Fixing what usually breaks in performance management

I did not set out to build software. I set out to solve the failures that quietly undermine most performance programs. Each of these is a well-known problem in the field, and each one drove a specific design choice.

Problem: inconsistent feedback behavior

Feedback that depends on which manager you happen to have

In most companies, feedback quality is a lottery. Some managers coach constantly, others go quiet until review season, and the feedback that does arrive is shaped by whatever happened in the last two weeks. I made the cadence structural rather than optional: light monthly check-ins with a simple color signal, plus evergreen peer feedback anyone can send at any time. Feedback becomes a steady habit the system prompts and logs, not something that rises and falls with individual manager diligence.

Problem: vague calibration and scoring

Ratings that mean different things to different people

A "3" from a strict manager and a "3" from a lenient one are not the same, yet calibration meetings often treat them as if they are, and the loudest advocate wins. I anchored scoring to shared, defined signals, then built an outlier view that plots each manager against the org average and flags anyone rating well outside it. Calibration now opens with evidence of grading bias on the table, and the workspace shows tenure and time-in-role on every card so promotion debates rest on context, not memory.

Problem: recency bias and thin documentation

Decisions written from memory, hard to defend later

When reviews are annual and written from scratch, they lean on the last month and leave little behind to justify a promotion or support a difficult conversation. Because feedback here is captured continuously and filed automatically, every review sits on top of a longitudinal record. Prior-quarter context surfaces right where a manager writes, and the full history is one click away, so decisions are grounded in the whole period and there is always a paper trail.

08 · Outcomes & Reflection

What shipped, and what it demonstrates

A two-person team stood up a performance program that a much larger company would recognize, in under three months, by treating automation as the default and human attention as the resource to protect.

The program now runs monthly check-ins and quarterly reviews end to end: cycles open, prompts and nudges fire in Slack, responses collect and file themselves, and People Ops works from a single live dashboard instead of chasing spreadsheets. Calibration, peer feedback, and compensation adjustments all read from the same source of truth, so nothing is re-entered by hand.

The system is the artifact. The transferable skill is the one underneath it: knowing what to build, what to automate, and what to leave to human judgment, then using AI to close the gap between a design and a working tool without waiting in an engineering queue.
